conservation of mass, eulerian

¶ in the eulerian approach to mass conservation, you monitor the change in density at a given point in space, rather than moving with the flow [thorne p692].

¶ when dealing with continua, all conservation of mass laws tend to have the form ˝𝝏₍t₎˝(density of something) = ˝∇⦁˝(flux of that thing). ie, rate of change of density wrt time = rate of change of flux wrt space

¶ conservation of mass, differential form:

££ \begin{equation} 𝝏₍t₎ρ + ∇⦁(ρ↗v) = 0 𝓮𝓺{221} \end{equation} ££

¶ any “newtonian” conservation law in differential form has a corresponding integral form [thorne p692]. you have to integrate the differential law over some arbitrary volume, call it ˝V’˝

££ \bgnmaths 𝝏₍t₎ ∫₍V'₎ ρ dV &= -∫₍V'₎ ∇⦁(ρ↗v) dV \n &= -∫₍S₎ ρ↗v⦁d↗S \endmaths ££

  • this is an application of gauss’ law
  • S is the closed surface that bounds ˝V’˝
  • LHS: the rate of change of mass in volume ˝V’˝
  • RHS: the rate at which mass flows into ˝V’˝ through surface ˝𝝏S˝
  • ˝ρ↗v˝ is the mass flux
  • ˝-d↗S˝ is the inward facing surface element

conservation of mass, lagrangian

¶ in the lagrangian approach to mass conservation, you look at the change in density as an observer who moves with the flow ˝↗v˝ [thorne p692]

¶ mass conservation, differential form, lagrangian:

££ \begin{equation} Ɗ₍t₎ρ = -ρ ∇⦁↗v \end{equation} ££
  • ˝Ɗ₍t₎˝ is the advective time derivative, ˝Ɗ₍t₎ = 𝝏₍t₎ + ↗v⦁∇˝
  • derivation: expand the second term in \eqref{221},
    ££ \begin{equation} ∇⦁(ρ↗v) = (∇ρ)⦁↗v + ρ∇⦁↗v \end{equation} ££
    ∴
    ££ \bgnmaths Ɗ₍t₎ρ &= 𝝏₍t₎ρ + ↗v⦁(∇ρ) \n &= -(∇ρ)⦁↗v - ρ∇⦁↗v + ↗v⦁(∇ρ) \endmaths ££
    the first and last terms cancel, so
    ££ \begin{equation} Ɗ₍t₎ρ = -ρ∇⦁↗v 𝓮𝓺{222} \end{equation} ££

¶ ˝Ɗ₍t₎˝, the advective time derivative,

££ \begin{equation} Ɗ₍t₎ = 𝝏₍t₎ + ↗v⦁∇ \end{equation} ££
  • physical interpretation?
  • note, the partial derivative ˝𝝏₍t₎˝ is the rate of change of something (XXX), wrt time, at a fixed point in space, ie you could express it more formally as ˝𝝏₍t₎ = 𝝏₍t₎|₍↗x₎˝
  • so, for a system in motion (a system changing with time), the partial derivative ˝𝝏₍t₎˝ compares the qty XXX at two different points in the flow, but at the same point in space. it compares qty XXX at location ˝↗x˝ at time ˝t˝ to qty XXX at location ˝↗x˝ at time ˝t + dt˝.
  • the advective derivative follows the motion. it compares XXX at two different times, at the same point in the material

¶ how the lagrangian approach works:

  • consider an block of fluid, volume ˝V˝
  • the mass in the block of fluid is ˝m = ρV˝
  • as the fluid flows, the mass must be conserved, so
    ££ \begin{equation} Ɗ₍t₎m = (Ɗ₍t₎ρ)·V + ρ·(Ɗ₍t₎V) = 0 \end{equation} ££
    or
    ££ \begin{equation} Ɗ₍t₎ρ = -÷ρV·Ɗ₍t₎V \end{equation} ££
  • now compare this with \eqref{222}, since ˝Ɗ₍t₎ρ = -ρ∇⦁↗v˝,
    ££ \begin{equation} ∇⦁↗v = ÷1V Ɗ₍t₎V \end{equation} ££
  • so the divergence of the flow velocity ˝↗v˝ is the rate of change of the fluid block’s volume divided by ˝V˝ (thorne calls this the “fractional rate of increase of the fluid block’s volume)

¶ define a fluid’s rate of expansion, ˝θ˝,

££ \begin{equation} θ = ∇⦁↗v = Ɗ₍t₎Θ \end{equation} ££

conservation of momentum

¶ for momentum conservation, the equation should be of form ˝𝝏₍t₎˝(momentum density)+ ˝∇⦁˝(momentum flux) = 0

¶ for fluids the momentum density is ˝ρv˝.

¶ the momentum flux is (generally) the stress tensor ˝T˝

¶ momentum conservation, eulerian

££ \begin{equation} 𝝏₍t₎(ρ↗v) + ∇⦁T = 0 \end{equation} ££
